Meditation:Some Scientific Evidence and How to

Posted on 06-10-2016   |     |   visitors

1. What is meditation

My definition for meditation:

It is a way of deliberate practice for the brain’s fitness.

Like building muscles in the gym, meditation is an effective way to train our brains. This conclusion could be supported by an increasing amount of scientific evidence. Here are two examples:

Our initial results suggest that meditation may be associated with structural changes in areas of the brain that are important for sensory, cognitive and emotional processing. The data further suggest that meditation may impact age-related declines in cortical structure. (Lazar et al., 2005)

Meditators, compared with controls, showed significantly greater cortical thickness in the anterior regions of the brain. These brain structural differences, particularly in the frontal cortex, may be associated with repeated practice of attentional and emotional regulations. (Kang et al.,2012)

Therefore, meditation could be independent from any religion and should be treated as another way to train our brains besides acquiring knowledge.

2. Why meditate

Let’s take Steve Jobs as an example, the founder of Apple. Today happens to be the fifth year we lost him.

Since Steve Jobs travelled India at around 19 years old, he had continued cultivating his meditation practice.

Editor Drake Baer mentioned three fundamental impacts brought from Jobs’s practice of this “discipline”.

  1. It transformed Jobs’s understanding of his own thinking procedure;
  2. It equipped Apple’s design principles with simplicity;
  3. It even guided the way how Jobs treated his customers.

Tech writer Geoffrey James summarised that Jobs used meditation to reduce stress, gain clarity and enhance his creativity.

According to a recent article in The Atlantic, giant companies such as Target, Google, Ford, Adobe — and even Goldman Sachs and Davos have developed similar programs to keep employees healthier and more focused.

3. My how-to guide

There are numerous guides on the Web about mindfulness and meditation. Here is a free and more systematic one.

My guide simply starts with focusing on our breath:

  1. Find a comfortable place and position, sitting with back straight;
  2. Close your eyes and take breaths at a natural pace;
  3. Focus on your breath;
  4. When your attention has wandered, simply return it to your breath;
  5. Practice at least 15 minutes daily and repetition is vital.

In addition, here are some apps could assist your practice: a timer, Headspace, the Breathe app (max 5-minute limit) on Apple Watch with watchOS 3.
